Illinois Street Bay Trail Greening

Greening and safety improvements along the Illinois Street Bay Trail corridor from Mariposa to Cargo Way — with street trees, native plantings, protected bike and pedestrian infrastructure, and a new pocket park on the PG&E parcel between 23rd and 24th Streets

Take Action to Support This Project

📍 Location

Corridor: Illinois Street from Mariposa to Cargo Way
Pocket Park Site: PG&E-owned parcel between 23rd and 24th Streets
Neighborhood: Dogpatch / Potrero Hill, San Francisco
Connects: Crane Cove Park, Power Station development, Heron's Head Park
Nearby: Warriors Chase Center, AIC Building, YMCA, Dogpatch neighborhood

🌱 Project Overview

Illinois Street from Mariposa to Cargo Way is a critical segment of the San Francisco Bay Trail — but today it's a bleak, treeless corridor dominated by heavy commercial traffic with little protection for people walking or biking. Empty tree wells have been paved over, crosswalks feel unsafe, and the stretch between Crane Cove Park and Heron's Head Park offers no green respite.

This project envisions transforming the entire Illinois Street corridor into a green, safe, and welcoming Bay Trail connection — with street trees, native plantings, protected bike and pedestrian infrastructure, stormwater management, and traffic calming. The project integrates with the Safer Illinois Street initiative to bring a protected, bidirectional bike lane and multi-use trail along the corridor.

A centerpiece of the corridor is a new pocket park on the PG&E-owned parcel between 23rd and 24th Streets. This massive site is currently dominated by hardscape, sharp decorative rocks designed to discourage use, and sparse non-native plantings — despite its size, it has no shade, no seating, and no reason for anyone to stop. The project would transform it into a genuine neighborhood green space with native plantings, tree canopy, community gathering areas, and a gateway to the adjacent Power Station development.

🎯 Why This Corridor Needs Action

  • Bay Trail Gap: Illinois Street from Mariposa to Cargo Way is a key Bay Trail segment connecting Crane Cove Park and Heron's Head Park, but today it's a bleak, unwelcoming stretch with no green infrastructure
  • No Street Trees: The corridor has virtually no street trees, with empty tree wells that have been paved back over
  • Unsafe for People: Illinois Street carries heavy commercial traffic alongside people biking, scooting, and walking with little protection; 31% of nearby households don't own a car
  • Hostile Design at PG&E Parcel: The massive PG&E-owned site between 23rd and 24th uses sharp rocks and barren hardscape intentionally designed to discourage people from using the space
  • Power Station Gateway: With the Power Station development bringing thousands of new residents, workers, and students, this corridor will be a primary entryway that deserves a welcoming green approach
  • Stormwater Management: Replacing hardscape with permeable surfaces and rain gardens addresses stormwater runoff while beautifying the corridor

🚲 Safer Illinois Street Integration

This greening project is tightly integrated with the Safer Illinois Street campaign to bring a high-quality, protected bikeway along the full corridor from Mariposa to Cargo Way:

  • Green-Protected Bike Lane: Native plantings and trees providing green buffering for the new bidirectional Bay Trail connector bike lane along the full corridor
  • Multi-Use Trail: A tree-lined walking and cycling path from Mariposa to Cargo Way, connecting Crane Cove Park to Heron's Head Park through a pleasant green corridor
  • Safer Crossings: Hardened intersection treatments along the corridor, integrated with stormwater curb extensions
  • Pocket Park Rest Stop: The PG&E parcel park provides a green rest stop and gathering place along the trail, with the bike lane running alongside

🌳 Vision: The Corridor and Pocket Park

  • Tree-Lined Corridor: Street trees along the full stretch of Illinois from Mariposa to Cargo Way, filling empty tree wells and creating a shaded, comfortable Bay Trail experience
  • Native Plantings: California native species along the corridor and throughout the pocket park, supporting pollinators and local ecology while replacing hostile rock landscaping
  • Protected Infrastructure: Green-buffered bike lane and widened pedestrian paths along the full corridor with safer crossings at every intersection
  • Pocket Park: Transform the PG&E parcel into a community green space with outdoor seating, picnic areas, and a welcoming plaza
  • Food Truck Plaza: A dedicated spot for the beloved neighborhood taco truck (currently a block away) to park on the plaza with outdoor seating
  • Active Recreation: Basketball hoops, exercise stations along the trail (the nearest basketball court is at Mission Channel under the highway), and possibly pickleball given proximity to the Warriors' Chase Center
  • Dog-Friendly Areas: With the adjacent dog hotel, designated off-leash space for neighborhood dogs to enjoy

⚡ PG&E Land & Ownership Context

The pocket park site between 23rd and 24th is PG&E-owned land, which adds complexity. The City of San Francisco and PG&E are currently in negotiations over potential transfer of PG&E assets to city ownership. While a transfer would simplify the pocket park component significantly, the broader corridor improvements on public right-of-way can proceed independently, and community advocacy for greening the PG&E parcel can proceed regardless of ownership status.

Even without a land transfer, PG&E community investment programs, city leverage through permitting, and Power Station developer contributions could fund pocket park improvements. The key is building a coalition that demonstrates strong community demand for transforming both the corridor and this space.
